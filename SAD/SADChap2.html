ANALYZING THE BUSINESS CASE

CC 207 A - System Analysis and Design

WHAT IS A BUSINESS CASE?

A business case refers to the reasons, or justification, for a proposal. It should be comprehensive, yet easy to understand. It should describe the project clearly, provide the justification to proceed, and estimate the project's financial impact.

Business Case refers to the reasons, or justification, for a proposal.

A complete business case should answer the following questions:

  • Why are we doing this project?
  • What is the project about?
  • How does this solution address key business issues?
  • How much will it cost?
  • How long will it take?
  • Will we suffer a productivity loss during the transition?
  • What is the return on investment and payback period?
  • What are the risks of doing the project?
  • What are the risks of not doing the project?
  • How will we measure success?
  • What alternatives exist?

Key Takeaway

Systems planning is the first phase of the systems development life cycle. A business case should describe the project clearly, provide the justification to proceed, and estimate the project's financial impact.

STRATEGIC PLANNING PROCESS

Strategic Planning is a dynamic process that starts with a mission statement, which is shaped by the firm's purpose, vision, and values.

Strategic Planning for IT Projects

Careful planning can help assure that:

  • The project supports overall business strategy and operational needs
  • The project scope is well-defined and clearly stated
  • The project goals are realistic, achievable, and tied to specific statements, assumptions, constraints, factors, and other inputs

Planning Tools

  • Some analysts use traditional text-based methods like Microsoft Word
  • Some analysts prefer a spreadsheet method like Microsoft Excel
  • The most effective approach is to use a CASE tool such as Visible Analyst

SWOT ANALYSIS

SWOT Analysis is a strategic planning tool that examines the Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, and Threats affecting an organization. It helps businesses understand their current position and plan for the future.

SWOT Analysis is an examination of a company's Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, and Threats.

An enterprise SWOT analysis usually begins with these questions:

  • What are our strengths, and how can we use them to achieve our business goals?
  • What are our weaknesses, and how can we reduce or eliminate them?
  • What are our opportunities, and how do we plan to take advantage of them?
  • What are our threats, and how can we assess, manage, and respond to the possible risks?

Example

A SWOT analysis might focus on a specific asset, such as a company patent, or on the entire organization. The analysis helps identify internal factors (strengths and weaknesses) and external factors (opportunities and threats) that affect the organization's strategies.

SYSTEMS REQUESTS AND REVIEW COMMITTEE

The systems development process starts with a systems request. These requests can come from various sources and for different reasons, and they need to be evaluated by appropriate parties.

Systems Request is a formal way to ask for IT support or services for a business need or opportunity.

Main Reasons for Systems Requests:

  • Improved Service: Improving service to customers or users within the company
  • Support for New Products and Services: New products and services often require new types or levels of IT support
  • Better Performance: Current system might not meet performance requirements

Factors That Affect Systems Projects

Internal Factors:

  • Strategic Plan
  • Top Managers
  • User Requests
  • Information Technology Department
  • Existing Systems and Data

External Factors:

  • Technology
  • Suppliers
  • Customers
  • Competitors
  • The Economy
  • Government

Systems Request Forms

An effective systems request form:

  • Streamlines the request process
  • Ensures consistency
  • Is easy to understand
  • Includes clear instructions
  • Indicates what supporting documents are needed
  • Can be submitted electronically

Advantages of using a committee

  • With a broader viewpoint, a committee can establish priorities more effectively than an individual
  • One person's bias is less likely to affect the decisions

Limitations of Review Committees

  • Action on requests must wait until the committee meets
  • Members might favor projects requested by their own departments
  • Internal political differences could delay important decisions

FEASIBILITY ANALYSIS

A feasibility study examines operational, technical, economic, and schedule factors to determine whether a project should be pursued. This analysis helps identify and filter out systems requests that are not feasible.

Feasibility Study is an analysis that determines the likelihood of a project's success based on operational, technical, economic, and schedule factors.

A feasibility study answers these key questions:

  • Is the proposal desirable in an operational sense?
  • Is the proposal technically feasible?
  • Is the proposal economically desirable?
  • Are other intangible factors involved, such as customer satisfaction or company image?
  • Can the proposal be accomplished within an acceptable time frame?

Operational Feasibility

  • Evaluates management and user support for the project.
  • Considers system adoption, workforce impact, and training needs.
  • Assesses risks to customer experience, company image, and legal/ethical concerns.
  • Determines operational changes and user involvement in planning.

Technical Feasibility

  • Examines availability of hardware, software, and network resources.
  • Evaluates system reliability, scalability, and performance expectations.
  • Assesses integration with internal and external systems.
  • Determines the necessity of a prototype.

Economic Feasibility

  • Analyzes costs for personnel, hardware, software, and training.
  • Considers expenses for licensing, consulting, and facilities.
  • Evaluates the financial impact of delaying or not developing the system.

Benefits to consider:

Tangible Benefits:

  • A new scheduling system that reduces overtime
  • An online package tracking system that improves service and decreases the need for clerical staff
  • A sophisticated inventory control system that cuts excess inventory and eliminates production delays

Intangible Benefits:

  • A user-friendly system that improves employee job satisfaction
  • A sales tracking system that supplies better information for marketing decisions
  • A new Web site that enhances the company's image

Schedule Feasibility

Key considerations:

  • Evaluates control over factors affecting project timeline.
  • Determines if management has set a clear timetable.
  • Identifies necessary conditions for system development.
  • Assesses risks of an accelerated schedule.
  • Ensures project management techniques are in place for coordination.
  • Confirms if a project manager will be assigned.

Purpose of Feasibility Analysis

Feasibility studies help to:

  • Identify and weed out systems requests that are not feasible
  • Determine necessity even if a project is feasible
  • Track requests that are not currently feasible but may be resubmitted as new hardware, software, or expertise becomes available

DETERMINING PROJECT PRIORITY

Even when projects are feasible, they must be prioritized based on various factors to determine which ones should be pursued first.

Factors That Affect Priority:

  • Will the proposed system reduce costs? Where? When? How? How much?
  • Will the system increase revenue for the company? Where? When? How? How much?
  • Will the systems project result in more information or produce better results? How? Are the results measurable?
  • Will the system serve customers better?
  • Will the system serve the organization better?
  • Can the project be implemented in a reasonable time period? How long will the results last?
  • Are the necessary financial, human, and technical resources available?

Discretionary Projects

Projects where management has a choice in implementing them.

Example: Creating a new report for a user

Nondiscretionary Projects

Projects where management must implement them.

Example: Adding a report required by federal law

Most of these projects are predictable:

  • Annual updates to payroll
  • Tax percentages
  • Quarterly changes

PRELIMINARY INVESTIGATION

A preliminary investigation is a critical part of the systems development process, helping to understand the problem, define project scope, gather facts, and determine feasibility before presenting recommendations to management.

Preliminary Investigation is an initial assessment of a systems request to determine the exact nature of the problem or opportunity and recommend specific action.

Steps in the Preliminary Investigation

The preliminary investigation consists of six main steps:

  1. Understand the problem or opportunity
  2. Define the project scope and constraints
  3. Perform fact-finding
  4. Analyze project usability, cost, benefit, and schedule data
  5. Evaluate feasibility
  6. Present results and recommendations to management

Step 1: Understand the Problem or Opportunity

  • Develop a business profile that describes business processes and functions
  • Understand how modifications will affect business operations and other information systems
  • Determine which departments, users, and business processes are involved
  • Systems request may not reveal an underlying problem
  • Consider using a fishbone diagram to identify actual causes rather than just symptoms

Step 2: Define the Project Scope and Constraints

  • Define the specific boundaries, or extent, of the project
  • Define project scope by creating a list with sections called Must Do, Should Do, Could Do, and Won't Do
  • Define project scope as clearly as possible to avoid project creep
  • Identify Constraints
  • A constraint is a requirement or condition that the system must satisfy or an outcome that the system must achieve

Step 3: Perform Fact-Finding

Gather data about project usability, costs, benefits, and schedules through various methods:

  • Analyze organization charts: Understand the functions and identify people you want to interview
  • Conduct Interviews:
    1. Determine the people to interview
    2. Establish objectives for the interview
    3. Develop interview questions
    4. Prepare for the interview
    5. Conduct the interview
    6. Document the interview
    7. Evaluate the interview
  • Review Documentation: Investigate the current system documentation and check with users to confirm accuracy and completeness
  • Observe Operations: See how workers carry out typical tasks and sample inputs and outputs of the system
  • Conduct a User Survey: A survey is not as flexible as interviews, but it is less expensive, generally takes less time, and can involve a broader cross-section of people
  • Analyze the Data: Use tools like Pareto charts and XY charts to identify issues and correlations

Step 4: Analyze Project Usability, Cost, Benefit, and Schedule Data

Key questions to consider:

  • What information must you obtain, and how will you gather and analyze the information?
  • Will you conduct interviews? How many people will you interview, and how much time will you need to meet with the people and summarize their responses?
  • Will you conduct a survey? Who will be involved? How much time will it take people to complete it? How much time will it take to tabulate the results?
  • How much will it cost to analyze the information and prepare a report with findings and recommendations?

Step 5: Evaluate Feasibility

  • OPERATIONAL FEASIBILITY: Review of user needs, requirements, and expectations. Look for areas that might present problems for system users and how they might be resolved.
  • TECHNICAL FEASIBILITY: Identify the hardware, software, and network resources needed to develop, install, and operate the system. Develop a checklist that will highlight technical costs and concerns.
  • ECONOMIC FEASIBILITY: Apply financial analysis tools. The cost-benefit data will be important.
  • SCHEDULE FEASIBILITY: Include stakeholder expectations regarding acceptable timing and completion dates.

Step 6: Present Results and Recommendations to Management

Typical Report Includes:

  • Introduction
  • Systems Request Summary
  • Findings
  • Case for Action
  • Project Roles
  • Time and Costs Estimates
  • Expected Benefits
  • Appendix
